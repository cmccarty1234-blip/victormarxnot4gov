Overview

Victor Marx (legal name: Vaughn Victor Kennedy) is a Republican candidate for Governor of Colorado (2026). He is the founder and president of All Things Possible Ministries (ATP), a 501(c)(3) nonprofit organization based in Colorado Springs, Colorado. Marx is a public speaker, author, and self-described humanitarian who focuses on anti-human trafficking advocacy, trauma recovery, child protection, and military veteran support. He is a former United States Marine and describes a Christian conversion experience as the defining turning point of his adult life.

Section 09
Legal Framework

This section explains the specific federal tax laws that govern related-party transactions at nonprofit organizations. Understanding these rules is necessary context for the legal analysis in Section 10.

9.1 · IRC §4958 — Excess Benefit Transactions

IRC §4958 imposes excise taxes on excess benefit transactions between applicable tax-exempt organizations (including 501(c)(3) organizations) and disqualified persons. An excess benefit transaction occurs when an economic benefit is provided by the organization directly or indirectly to a disqualified person, and the value of the economic benefit provided exceeds the value of the consideration received by the organization. [IRC §4958(c)(1)(A)]

An excise tax of 25% of the excess benefit is imposed on the disqualified person. If the transaction is not corrected timely, an additional tax of 200% may apply. Organization managers who knowingly participate in the transaction without reasonable cause may be liable for a 10% tax (capped at $20,000 per transaction). [IRC §4958(a), (b), (d)]

9.2 · Definition of Disqualified Person

A disqualified person includes any person who was, at any time during the five-year period ending on the date of the transaction, in a position to exercise substantial influence over the affairs of the organization. [IRC §4958(f)(1)] This expressly includes:

  • Founders, presidents, chief executive officers, chief operating officers, treasurers, or chief financial officers. [Treas. Reg. §53.4958-3(c)(2)]
  • Members of the governing body (i.e., board members), regardless of title. [Treas. Reg. §53.4958-3(c)(2)]
  • Family members of disqualified persons, including spouses and children. [IRC §4958(f)(1)(B); Treas. Reg. §53.4958-3(b)(1)]
  • Entities in which a disqualified person holds more than 35% ownership or beneficial interest, including corporations, partnerships, and trusts. [IRC §4958(f)(3); Treas. Reg. §53.4958-3(b)(2)]
9.3 · Form 990 Schedule L — Disclosure Requirements

Schedule L (Transactions with Interested Persons) requires disclosure of:

  • Part I: Excess benefit transactions under §4958 (if any).
  • Part II: Loans to or from interested persons, including officers, directors, trustees, key employees, and 35%-or-more controlled entities.
  • Part III: Grants or assistance to interested persons.
  • Part IV: Business transactions with interested persons exceeding the greater of $10,000 or 1% of total revenues for compensation arrangements, and $100,000 for other transactions.
9.4 · Rebuttable Presumption of Reasonableness

Under Treas. Reg. §53.4958-6, a transaction is presumed to be at fair market value (and thus not an excess benefit transaction) if three procedural safeguards are satisfied:

  • (1) The transaction was approved by an authorized body of the organization, composed entirely of individuals who did not have a conflict of interest with respect to the transaction;
  • (2) Before making its determination, the authorized body obtained and relied upon appropriate comparability data; and
  • (3) The authorized body adequately documented the basis for its determination concurrently with making the determination (e.g., in meeting minutes).
Failure to satisfy all three prongs does not automatically establish an excess benefit transaction, but it shifts the burden to the organization to demonstrate reasonableness — a significantly more difficult evidentiary task in an IRS examination.
